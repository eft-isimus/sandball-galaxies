RANDOM WALKS

What is a random walker?

To avoid the cliche of the drunk man struggling to get home, let us consider a lost crab trying to get back to its burrow. If we were to be cruel enough to displace a small sand-bubbler crab by picking it up from next to its burrow, and placing it far, far away, it likely has no idea of how to get back home. In its confusion, it is as likely to step in one direction as in any other. In such a case, it is of course impossible for us to accurately predict the path that the crab will take, because every step it takes is random. But we can still model this kind of motion using what we call a random walk.

Let us first simplify things by assuming that the crab's movement is restricted to a square grid, meaning

  1. it can only ever take a step forward, backward, to the left, or to the right,
  2. and the length of each step is the same.

We must also remember an important assumption we made earlier, that the crab is just as likely to pick each direction, which means there is a 1/4 chance of it picking any of the four possible directions to take a step in.

We define our coordinate system such that the origin \((x,y) = (0, 0)\) is the point where the crab is dropped off after being rudely abducted. At any given time, the distance the crab has travelled from this point is \(R(N) = \sqrt{x(N)^2 + y(N)^2}\) , where N is the number of steps. We write x, y and R as functions of N because they change with every step that the crab takes, and thus depend on the number of steps it has taken thus far. We can also define a different but related value, the vector \(\mathbf{R(N)}\) (vectors are usually marked in bold-face), which has both a magnitude, which is \(R(N)\), and a direction, which depends on the x and y values specifically.

Intuitively, we might expect that as the number of steps increases, the crab gets farther and farther away from its starting position. Let us see if we can arrive at this mathematically by trying to calculate \(\langle \mathbf{R(N)}\rangle\)1, (\footnote{The angular brackets around any quantity represent an average of some sort.}) Here, we can imagine that the average is taken over the paths of many, many crabs who have been displaced from their burrows.}) which represents the average of the vector \(\mathbf{R(N)}\). Now, let us consider what happens to the two position coordinates of the crab.

1 The angular brackets around any quantity represent an average of that quantity (what ensemble the average is over may need to be defined more clearly depending on the context).

A general prescription for calculating averages is as follows: we multiply a possible value of the variable with the probability that this value will occur. We then do this for all possible values, and take the sum of the resulting quantities. This is represented as follows:

\[ \sum_i P(x_i)\cdot x_i \]

In our case, \(x\) can take the values +1 and -1. But since the probability for each of these steps is the same (1/4), this quantity simply gives us a zero. Similarly, the average for the position in y also gives us a zero. This must mean that the average, \(\langle \mathbf{R(N)}\rangle\) must be zero! But does this mean that our intuition about the crab moving farther and farther away from its origin with each step is completely wrong? Not really! To see this, we can calculate the average of a slightly different quantity \(\langle R^2(N)\rangle\), which is the square of the distance moved in N steps.

\[ Calculation of \langle R^2(N)\rangle \]

We call this the mean squared displacement, and the average distance travelled by the crab is the square root of this quantity! But why is this quantity non-zero when the average of the vector \(\mathbf{R(N)}\) is zero?

We can interpret this in the following way. If we look at two crabs who have both taken a 100 steps, let us assume that both of them have travelled exactly the same distance R(N), but one has moved towards the left and downward from the origin, and the towards the right and upward. If we now take an average of the vector \(\mathbf{R(N)}\), we will find it to be zero, since the two vectors will perfectly cancel each other out! And even though we have picked a specific example to do this, this is generally true, because every possible value of the \(\mathbf{R(N)}\) will have a corresponding value that exactly cancels it out! But this does not mean that the average distance moved by the crab after a 100 steps is zero. In fact, if we were to measure the final position of many crabs, and plot each of these on a graph, we would find that there will be a ring of points at a radius \(N^2\) from the centre where the crabs are the likeliest to end up!

Instead of being cruel and displacing thousands of actual frogs to verify whether all of our calculations are correct, we can create tiny virtual crabs which can be abducted and displaced without it weighing on our conscience. To create one such crab, we store the position of the crab (x, y), and then simply draw a random number between 0 and 1 for each step. If the number is below 0.25, we take a step upward, if it is between 0.25 and 0.5, we go downward, and so on. Assuming our random number has an equal chance to be anywhere between 0 and 1, or in other words, is drawn from a uniform probability distribution, this would mean at each step our virtual crab is equally likely to go in any of the four possible directions. We then simulate an N-step walk, and repeat this a large number of times in order to obtain the statistics for the system.
